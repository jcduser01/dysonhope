I go to clubs by myself most of the time, and I don't find it strange at all.

Part of that is circumstance. Through much of my life, I spent most of the year outside the country that issued my passport, and the thing I'm chasing — new music, mixed well — doesn't keep convenient hours or wait for a group to assemble. So if I wanted to be in the room when it's good, I'd go. Alone, usually. But circumstance isn't really the reason. The reason is that going alone was never a compromise for me. It might even be the point.

Back in the rave days in the mid-90s, I'd go to raves by myself, which nobody did then. It only looked solitary from the outside. I was out so often that I'd find friends once I got there, so I wasn't really alone in any way that mattered. That changed slowly. The scene got pushed indoors into clubs. The big, loose crowd of acquaintances thinned to a handful of actual friends, and then those friends started having kids and jobs and the kind of life that doesn't include a late-night Tuesday. One by one, they stopped coming. And I kept going.

There's an old line about why people go to bars. Guys go to get drunk and flirt with girls; girls go to get drunk and flirt with guys. It's crude, and it leaves a lot of people out, but it's honest about why most of the room is there. That was never my reason. My first reason, my last reason, and most nights my only reason, was the music. I've stood in venues with a tuned Funktion-One rig, a real lighting design, good cheap drinks poured by people who knew what they were doing, and a floor full of beautiful people, and left early because the music was wrong. None of the rest of it could hold me. I'd rather be in a dingy basement with one strobe, no bar, and a DJ tearing the roof off with two decks on a card table. Give me that over the spectacle every time, because the spectacle was never the thing I came for.

So the company was never the draw. The music was. Once you know that about yourself, going alone stops being a thing you tolerate and becomes the cleanest way to do the thing you actually came for. No one to manage, no one's night to worry about but your own. Just you and the room and whoever's behind the decks, deciding together whether tonight is any good.

That's the part worth saying to anyone, dance music or not. Most of us treat doing something alone as the consolation prize — the version you settle for when no one's free. But if you're honest about what you're actually there for, alone is sometimes the better seat. You stop performing the outing and start having it.
